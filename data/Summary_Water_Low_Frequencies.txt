Comprehensive Technical Summary: Water at Low Frequencies and Vibrational Dynamics
Source Authors: J. P. Martinez Cordeiro and N. R. Aluru (2026)

Overview
This research investigates the low-frequency vibrational dynamics of water (0 to 1000 cm^-1) to understand how different environments—such as ionic concentration, confinement, surface charge, and dynamic surfaces—affect its hydrogen bonding and collective motions. The researchers utilize classical molecular dynamics simulations to perform a mode-resolved analysis of the Vibrational Density of States (VDOS), decomposing it into specific translational and rotational components. A major finding is the identification of distinct spectroscopic signatures tied to surface charge polarity, offering a theoretical basis for experimentalists to infer surface charge from interfacial spectroscopic measurements.

Methodology
The computational approach relies on the Vibrational Density of States (VDOS), defined as the mass-weighted velocity power spectral density. This allows for the decomposition of vibrational modes into individual degrees of freedom by projecting atomic velocities onto a local molecular reference frame (dipole vector, normal to molecular plane, and an in-plane vector orthogonal to the others). The simulations were conducted using LAMMPS, placing water (modeled primarily with SPC/E, though validated across multiple models like TIP4P) between two frozen graphene sheets. To study charged surfaces, uniform charge densities (ranging from +/-0.015 to +/-0.24 C/m^2) were distributed on the graphene. For surface-driven perturbations, the neutral graphene was oscillated harmonically out-of-plane.

Key Technical Findings

1. VDOS Decomposition of Bulk Water
The researchers identified that the hydrogen-bond (HB) bending mode (approx. 50 cm^-1) is quasi-isotropic and heavily dominated by translational motion along all three principal axes. In contrast, the HB-stretching mode (approx. 200 cm^-1) is highly directional, mostly composed of translational movements along the molecular plane, combined with a significant librational (hindered rotational) background. The librational mode peaks broadly around 500 cm^-1.

2. Impact of Ionic Concentration and Confinement
Increasing ionic concentration (e.g., KCl) leads to a blue shift in the water-specific HB-bending peak, largely along the dipole direction, due to the orientational preference of hydration shell molecules. Surface confinement alone induces a blue shift (approx. 10 cm^-1) in HB-bending and a red shift (approx. 25 cm^-1) in librational modes, reflecting altered hydrogen bond constraints near the wall.

3. Signatures of Surface Charge Polarity
The study reveals that surface charge significantly modulates interfacial water dynamics. Negative surface charges induce a red shift in both HB-bending and librational modes, as molecules orient with a hydrogen atom toward the surface, leading to less constrained out-of-plane motion. Conversely, positive surface charges impose a flatter orientation, constraining out-of-plane translation and causing a blue shift. Both charges blue-shift the HB-stretching mode. The massive shifts in the librational peak (up to approx. 65 cm^-1) provide a promising, measurable fingerprint for determining surface polarity via advanced spectroscopy (e.g., VSFG, ATR-IR).

4. Effects of Dynamic Surface Perturbations
When the confining graphene is subjected to out-of-plane rigid oscillations, the perturbations couple most intensely with translational modes rather than rotational ones. Resonance is explicitly observed at approx. 50 cm^-1, aligning perfectly with the quasi-isotropic HB-bending mode. Energy injected at this frequency dissipates rapidly into both translational and librational modes, generating localized "heating" at the interface.
